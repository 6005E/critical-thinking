# Strategic and Planning Tasks

Load when the task involves resource allocation decisions, business strategy, multi-step planning, constraint analysis, or any situation where the primary output is a course of action under real constraints.

---

## Constraint Identification

- Before evaluating a proposed action, identify the binding constraint — the factor whose relief would most change the outcome. The salient problem is not always the binding constraint. Proposed actions that address non-binding constraints consume resources without changing the outcome.
- When time and capital are both constrained, identify which is more binding first. Actions that trade one for the other (spending capital to save time, or delaying to conserve capital) must be evaluated against the actual binding limit.
- When a plan is proposed under a resource constraint, calculate what the plan does to that constraint before evaluating what it achieves. An action that extends capability while shrinking runway may produce a shorter window in which the capability matters.

---

## Goal-Action Alignment

- When a proposed action consumes the primary resource needed to achieve the goal, flag this as a goal-action conflict before proceeding. The most common form: spending money to generate revenue faster, where the spend reduces the time available for the revenue to materialize.
- Distinguish between actions that address the binding constraint and actions that address a symptom of it. An action that improves a non-binding factor while leaving the binding constraint unchanged does not move the outcome.

---

## Load-Bearing Premises

- Every plan rests on at least one assumption whose failure would invalidate the plan. Identify it explicitly before evaluating the plan. Ask: what has to be true for this action to produce the desired outcome within the available window?
- When the load-bearing premise is unverified, assess the cost of being wrong before recommending the action. If the premise fails and the action has consumed significant resources, is recovery possible?

---

## Alternatives and Option Space

- Before recommending a single lever, enumerate the full option space: what other actions could address the same constraint, and what do they cost? Plans presented as binary (do this or fail) almost always have unexplored alternatives.
- When enumerating alternatives, generate candidates across the full range of intervention types — reducing the constraint, changing the goal, buying time, finding external resources, or redefining scope — before recommending any one of them.

---

## Working Backwards

- When a plan is aimed at reaching a milestone (fundraising, revenue, launch), work backwards from the milestone: what must be true at the milestone for it to be achieved, and does the proposed action produce those conditions within the available window?
- If the proposed action produces results that arrive after the constraint binds (runway exhausted, deadline passed), it does not serve the goal regardless of its quality.

---

## Irreversibility and Recovery Cost

- When evaluating whether to accept an opportunity, assess the cost of failure independently from the cost of declining. If failure after acceptance is significantly more costly than declining — reputational damage, contractual obligations, staff hired and then released, community commitments — weight the decision accordingly. Opportunities with non-recoverable failure modes require a higher confidence threshold than recoverable ones.
- When an opportunity comes with externally imposed conditions — timeline, scope, reporting requirements, performance milestones — treat those conditions as fixed constraints unless negotiation has been confirmed possible. Evaluate the opportunity against the actual terms, not idealized ones.
- When a decision is framed as requiring immediate action, check whether the urgency is real or constructed. Identify specifically what becomes irreversible if the decision is delayed — if the answer is nothing concrete, the urgency is artificial and is suppressing option enumeration and premise checks.
- When an external party takes an action framed as requiring a response, interpret the signal before deciding how to respond. What were their constraints, what response are they trying to produce, and does that response serve their interests at the cost of yours? Responding without reading the signal may produce exactly the outcome the initiator intended.
