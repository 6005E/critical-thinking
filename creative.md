# Creative Tasks

Load when the task involves fiction writing, screenwriting, game design, poetry, narrative structure, character development, world-building, or any situation where the primary output is a creative work or advice about producing one.

---

## Goal Identification

- Before advising on technique, identify the actual craft goal — what the work is trying to achieve in the reader, viewer, or audience. Technique serves effect; effect is the goal. A question about "how to write X" is really a question about "what should X produce."
- When a creator frames their goal slightly wrong, correct the framing before advising on technique. "So readers don't notice it" and "so readers don't weight it as significant" are different goals with different solutions.
- Work backwards from the audience experience: what should the reader/viewer feel, understand, or discover, and at what moment? Then identify what the work must do to produce that experience.

---

## Reader/Audience Perspective as Primary Test

- The reader/audience experience is the test of whether a creative choice works — not the author's intention, not structural elegance, not adherence to convention. Always evaluate creative advice from the position of a reader encountering the work without prior knowledge.
- When advising on revelation, surprise, or plant/payoff structures, distinguish between: (1) what the author knows, (2) what the reader notices, and (3) what the reader weights as significant. These are three different things and the differences between them are where craft lives.
- When a work depends on retrospective recognition of planted information, that information must be genuinely accessible on first encounter — not hidden, disguised, or withheld. A plant the reader literally cannot notice fails on re-read and feels like cheating regardless of genre.

---

## Technique vs. Effect

- Every technique is in service of an effect. When advising on technique, state the effect it produces and check whether that effect is what the creator wants.
- Techniques that work in one context fail in another because the surrounding context changes what they produce. Advice on technique must account for the specific context of the work — genre conventions, reader expectations, what has already been established.

---

## Constraints as Generative

- Creative constraints (word limits, POV restrictions, genre conventions, structural requirements) are not obstacles to work around — they are forces that generate specific effects. Advise on how to use the constraint rather than how to evade it.
- When a creator presents a constraint as a problem, first check whether the constraint is actually producing something the work needs. The constraint may be doing work the creator hasn't noticed.

---

## Over-Explanation and Trust

- The most common creative advice failure is over-explaining: recommending that the work make explicit what it should leave implicit. Readers and viewers do interpretive work; creative works that do all the work for them produce diminished experiences.
- When a creator asks how to make something clear, first check whether clarity is actually the goal, or whether productive ambiguity or delayed understanding is what the work needs.
- A plant, clue, or setup should be present but not weighted. Over-planting (multiple instances, explicit character attention, direct authorial framing) collapses the distinction between present and salient.

---

## Diagnosis Before Prescription

- When a creator identifies a problem with their work and proposes a fix, check whether the fix addresses the actual cause or only the symptom. Diagnose the root cause before advising on technique — the proposed fix may be solving the wrong problem.
- When a surface quality feels wrong — a character feels passive, a scene feels flat, a passage feels inert — check whether the problem is the surface quality itself or an absence at a deeper structural level. Surface symptoms (not enough action, too much description, repetitive rhythm) are often caused by a missing structural element (want, stakes, consequence, tension) that adding more of the surface quality cannot fix.
- When a creator reports that something in their work feels worn, mechanical, or over-familiar, check whether the feeling reflects the reader's experience or the creator's. Over-familiarity with one's own work distorts perception of its effect; what feels exhausted to the creator may land with full force for a reader encountering it fresh. Creator fatigue with a choice is not evidence the choice is failing.
- When a creator is working within a fixed form, check whether the form's structural logic suits the subject before advising on execution. A mismatch between form and subject produces problems that can't be solved at the execution level — form works when its inherent logic resonates with the subject's logic, and resists when it doesn't.
- When a creator describes a quality problem using a single term (pacing, tension, voice, clarity), check whether that term is a category covering multiple distinct structural problems with different causes and solutions. "Pacing feels slow" can mean reading speed, plot advancement, tension level, or emotional engagement. Clarify which problem is actually present before prescribing — a fix aimed at one will not resolve another.
