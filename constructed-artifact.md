# Constructed Artifact

Load when Q3 fires in triage: the primary output is a non-fiction artifact the user will deploy — an argument, explanation, positioning statement, briefing document, speech, or similar constructed deliverable.

These instructions are upstream — they fire before the artifact is produced, not during or after. For downstream quality checks on recommendations and plans, see output-quality.md. For evaluation of existing arguments, see argument-structure.md. For creative works, see creative.md.

---

## Framing Enumeration

- Before committing to a framing, enumerate at least two structurally distinct alternatives. Structurally distinct means different in kind — different burden of proof, different audience entry point, different causal structure — not merely different in tone or emphasis.
- Evaluate each framing against the goal and the audience before selecting or synthesizing. State which framing was selected and why — the selection reasoning is part of the output quality, not internal scaffolding.
- The most expected framing is frequently the weakest in competitive or high-stakes contexts. When the audience has already encountered the obvious framing, it has already discounted it. A framing that meets the audience where they are — at their existing belief, resistance, or fatigue — is typically stronger than one that asks them to accept a new premise before engaging.
- Synthesis is often stronger than selection: two framings that address different audience entry points can be combined into a single artifact that works at both levels.

## Audience and Use Modeling

- Before drafting, identify who will encounter this artifact and in what context. The person asking is not always the audience — a briefing written for a manager will be read by the manager's peers; a speech drafted for a CEO will be heard by employees. Calibrate for the actual audience, not the requester.
- Identify what the audience already believes, where they are likely to resist, and what would constitute success from their position. An artifact calibrated for a converted audience fails on a skeptical one; an artifact that treats a hostile audience as neutral will misread every response.
- Identify how the artifact will be used: read once, referenced repeatedly, shared onward, presented aloud, or acted on immediately. Each use context changes what the artifact must do — a one-time read prioritizes immediate comprehension; a reference document prioritizes findability and self-containment; a speech prioritizes pacing and oral rhythm.

---

## Burden of Proof Structure

- Identify who bears the burden of proof before drafting. In most persuasive contexts the burden falls on the party proposing change — but this is not universal, and accepting a default burden assignment without examining it is a framing error.
- When the burden can be shifted — by reframing what counts as the default, what counts as the status quo, or who is actually proposing the change — examine whether that shift serves the argument before accepting the conventional assignment.
- Structure the artifact to discharge the burden it has accepted. An artifact that claims more than its evidence supports, or that accepts a burden it cannot discharge, fails regardless of how well it is written.

---

## Counterargument Anticipation

- Identify the strongest case against the artifact's central claim before drafting. A constructed argument that hasn't survived its own steelman is not ready to deploy.
- Decide at the drafting stage whether to address the counterargument directly within the artifact or to construct the artifact so the counterargument doesn't get purchase. These are different structural choices with different implications — direct engagement signals confidence; structural inoculation signals that the counterargument has been anticipated without amplifying it.
- Do not omit counterarguments because they are strong. A strong counterargument omitted from the artifact will be raised by the audience; one addressed within the artifact is controlled by the author.

---

## Internal Coherence

- When an artifact has multiple parts — sections, paragraphs, premises, moves — verify that each part serves the central claim and that the parts add up to a whole. Local correctness at the part level does not guarantee global coherence at the artifact level.
- The opening should establish what the artifact is doing and why the audience should continue. The closing should do something with what has been established — not merely summarize it. If the closing is a restatement of the opening, the artifact has not moved.
- Check the inferential chain: does each section follow from the prior, and does the conclusion follow from the accumulated argument rather than being asserted at the end? A conclusion that could have been stated in the opening without loss is a sign the body isn't doing argumentative work.
