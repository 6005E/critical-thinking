# Interpersonal and Communication Tasks

Load when the task involves advice on delivering difficult messages, navigating conversations, managing relationships, giving feedback, or any situation where the primary output will shape a human interaction.

---

## Goal and Framing

- When someone asks "what should I say," check whether the actual need is language or whether it is guidance on conducting the interaction well. These are different deliverables. A script without delivery, timing, and follow-through guidance often fails the underlying goal.
- Work backwards from the recipient's need, not forward from the speaker's intent. The recipient's need — clarity, dignity, agency, safety, understanding — should organize the response. The speaker's comfort is not the goal.
- Treat conversations as processes, not transactions. What happens before (preparation, timing, who else knows) and after (follow-up, support, next steps) matters as much as the words used in the moment.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Optimizing for speaker comfort over recipient need.** Softening, delaying, or hedging information often serves the speaker's discomfort rather than the recipient's interest. Clarity at the right moment, even if uncomfortable to deliver, serves the recipient better than gentleness that obscures.
- **Performative acknowledgment as deflection.** Scripted empathy ("I know this is hard") before the person has had a chance to respond treats the emotional reaction as something to be managed rather than received. Acknowledgment should follow the recipient's response, not precede it.
- **Institutional language as avoidance.** Passive constructions and organizational framing ("the role has been eliminated," "a decision was made") can be accurate but can also read as evasion. Check whether the language serves clarity or protects the speaker.
- **Treating finality as negotiable.** If a decision is final, the language must make that clear — ambiguity invites false hope and damages trust when the finality becomes apparent later.
- **Skipping follow-through.** Advice on what to say is incomplete without addressing what comes immediately after: who else the person should speak to, what resources are available, what the timeline is, and when follow-up will happen.

---

## Structural Checks

- What does the recipient need to know, feel, and be able to do after this conversation? Has the advice served all three?
- Is any part of the recommended approach serving the speaker's comfort rather than the recipient's need? If so, flag it explicitly.
- Has the advice addressed the before (preparation, timing, setting), during (language, tone, listening), and after (follow-up, support, next steps)?
- If the recipient is likely to have a strong emotional reaction, has the advice accounted for that — not by suppressing it but by creating space for it?

---

## Multi-Party Conflict

- When advising a third party on conflict between two people, check whether enforced symmetry is warranted or whether it distorts a situation that isn't symmetric. Treating all accounts as equally valid by default protects more problematic behavior when the situation is actually asymmetric. The goal is accuracy, not neutrality.
- In multi-party conflicts, advise separate conversations before joint ones. A joint meeting before the mediator understands each party's perspective individually risks escalation, public defensiveness, and performative reconciliation that doesn't hold.
- When conflict accounts use pattern language, distinguish the behavioral incidents underlying the pattern from the interpretive narrative built on top of them before advising on intervention. Intervening on the narrative rather than the incidents risks reinforcing the account of whoever frames things more persuasively rather than addressing what actually happened.
- When the advice-seeker has a stake in the outcome of the conflict, flag this explicitly. Their stake may be shaping how they've framed the situation and what resolution they're actually seeking — which may differ from what they've stated.

---

## Information Transfer vs. Behavioral Change

- When a conversation is aimed at changing someone's behavior, check whether the person already knows what they're doing and why it's a problem. If they do, the conversation is not an information transfer — it's a change conversation. These require different structures: information transfer requires clarity; behavioral change requires diagnosing why the behavior is occurring and what would need to change for it to stop. Treating a change conversation as information delivery produces defensiveness rather than change, because the recipient experiences criticism rather than problem-solving.
