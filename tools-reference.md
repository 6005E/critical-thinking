# Tools Reference

Load when uncertain which reasoning approach fits, when facing complex tradeoffs, or when a structured framework would improve the response. Select by condition, not by name.

---

## When reasoning under uncertainty or with incomplete information

**Bayesian reasoning** — New evidence has arrived that should change the conclusion. Update beliefs explicitly: what was the prior, what does the evidence suggest, how should the conclusion change? Do not anchor to the original assessment.

**Fermi estimation** — Precise data is unavailable. Break the unknown into estimable components, multiply through, and check whether the result is plausible. Useful for sanity-checking conclusions and surfacing hidden assumptions about scale.

---

## When evaluating your own position or proposal

**Red teaming** — Confidence is high or a proposal is about to be presented. Actively argue against your own position: what would a well-informed critic say? Identify the strongest case against your conclusion before presenting it.

**Premortem** — A plan or solution is being proposed. Imagine it has already failed and reason about what caused it. Surfaces risks that forward-looking analysis misses.

---

## When optimizing or diagnosing

**Theory of constraints** — A system is being optimized or a bottleneck is suspected. Every system has exactly one limiting factor — identify it before optimizing anything else. Optimizing non-constraints wastes effort.

**Causal analysis** — A problem needs diagnosis or a cause needs identification. Trace cause and effect chains systematically. Don't stop at proximate causes — ask why until the root cause is reached. Don't mistake correlation for causation.

---

## When classifying a problem

**Cynefin framework** — The appropriate approach to a problem is unclear. Classify first:
- *Clear*: known solution exists → apply best practice
- *Complicated*: requires expert analysis → analyze then act
- *Complex*: no predetermined solution → probe, observe, adapt
- *Chaotic*: immediate action needed to stabilize → act first, then sense

Applying expert analysis to a complex problem, or waiting to probe when a clear solution exists, wastes effort or causes harm.

---

## When generating or transferring solutions

**Analogical reasoning** — The problem seems novel but may resemble a solved problem in a different domain. Actively seek structural similarity across apparently unrelated fields. Ask: what domain has solved something with this shape, and what would transfer?

**Inversion and transformation** — The solution space feels narrow or the first viable option is about to be accepted. For any element of the problem — component, process, assumption, role, relationship, sequence, or constraint — explicitly consider inverting or transforming its defining properties as a candidate. Properties that can be inverted or transformed include: direction, polarity, sequence, role, scale, scope, and relationship between elements. Apply this at every level of the problem, not just the system as a whole.

**Outside view estimation** — When estimating duration, cost, or effort, apply an outside view: how long do similar tasks actually take in practice, rather than how long does this one seem like it should take? Inside-view estimates are almost always too optimistic. Look for reference class data before committing to a timeline or budget.
